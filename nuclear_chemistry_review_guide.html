Nuclear Chemistry Review Guide for Regents

Types of radioactive decay
Alpha (α)
Alpha decay
Nucleus releases a helium nucleus (2 protons + 2 neutrons). Mass number drops by 4, atomic number drops by 2.
²²⁶₈₈Ra → ⁴₂He + ²²²₈₆Rn
Beta (β⁻)
Beta decay
Nucleus releases an electron. Atomic number increases by 1, mass number stays the same.
²³⁴₉₀Th → ⁰₋₁e + ²³⁴₉₁Pa
Positron (β⁺)
Positron decay
Nucleus releases a positron (positive electron). Atomic number decreases by 1, mass number stays the same.
³⁷₁₉K → ⁰₊₁e + ³⁷₁₈Ar
Gamma (γ)
Gamma decay
Nucleus releases high-energy electromagnetic radiation. No mass, no charge — nothing changes in the atomic number or mass number.

Half-life
Half-life = time for half the sample to decay. It never changes regardless of conditions.
Each period cuts the remaining amount in half. Count the steps, then divide total time by the number of steps.
100 g→ 50 g→ 25 g→ 12.5 g = 3 half-lives
Example problemSolution
100 g → 12.5 g in 90 years. What is the half-life? 3 half-life periods passed (100→50→25→12.5).
90 ÷ 3 = 30 years
What % of I-131 remains after 32 days? (half-life = 8 days) 32 ÷ 8 = 4 half-lives. 100% → 50% → 25% → 12.5% → 6.25%

Transmutation
Natural
Natural transmutation
An isotope spontaneously turns into a different element through normal radioactive decay. Only one reactant on the left side of the equation.
³⁷₁₉K → ⁰₊₁e + ³⁷₁₈Ar
Artificial
Artificial transmutation
The nucleus is bombarded with a particle to force it to transform. Two reactants on the left side.
²⁷₁₃Al + ⁴₂He → ³⁰₁₅P + ¹₀n
Nuclear reactions: fission vs fusion
Fission — split
A large nucleus is hit by a neutron and splits into two smaller nuclei, releasing more neutrons + huge amounts of energy. The extra neutrons trigger more fissions — this is a chain reaction. Used in atomic bombs and nuclear reactors.
²³⁵₉₂U + ¹₀n → ¹⁴¹₅₆Ba + ⁹²₃₆Kr + 3¹₀n + energy
Fusion — join
Two light nuclei combine to form one heavier nucleus, releasing enormous energy. Requires tremendous activation energy because both nuclei are positively charged and repel each other.
²₁H + ²₁H → ⁴₂He + energy

In both reactions, some mass is converted into energy — this is why they release so much more energy than chemical reactions.

Key definitions to know
TermMeaning
Unstable nucleusA nucleus with an unfavorable neutron-to-proton ratio that breaks down by releasing particles or energy
IsotopeAtoms of the same element (same protons) with different numbers of neutrons — some isotopes are stable, others are not
RadioisotopeAn unstable (radioactive) isotope that undergoes decay
Half-lifeTime for half the mass of a radioisotope to decay; stays constant regardless of temperature, pressure, or other conditions
TransmutationWhen an isotope turns into an isotope of a different element due to nuclear decay
FissionSplitting a large nucleus into smaller ones, releasing energy and more neutrons
FusionCombining two small nuclei into one larger nucleus, releasing energy
Chain reactionWhen neutrons from one fission trigger more fissions in a self-sustaining reaction (used in atomic bombs and reactors)
